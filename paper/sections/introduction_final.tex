Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning~\citep{wei2022chain} has become the dominant paradigm for improving LLM performance on complex tasks.
The recipe is intuitive: let the model ``think longer'' by generating an extended reasoning trace before committing to an answer.
This paradigm, operationalized in systems like OpenAI o1~\citep{openai2024o1}, QwQ~\citep{qwq2024}, and DeepSeek-R1~\citep{deepseekr1}, has produced impressive gains on mathematical reasoning benchmarks and is increasingly viewed as a reliable lever for scaling test-time performance~\citep{snell2024scaling}.
The implicit promise is simple: more thinking tokens, more accuracy.

We show that this promise breaks down whenever the output-token budget falls \emph{below a task- and model-specific crossover}---and that this crossover is far higher than commonly assumed.
The phenomenon requires two conditions: (i)~the model's reasoning chains typically exceed the budget, causing truncation waste; and (ii)~the model has an architecturally distinct non-thinking mode that can answer without generating a reasoning trace.

\paragraph{The thinking tax.}
Through systematic experiments on GSM8K and MATH-500 with three Qwen-family model sizes (8B, 9B, 27B)---all possessing native \texttt{<think>} mode with a distinct non-thinking alternative---we uncover a striking phenomenon that we term the \emph{thinking tax}: \textbf{below the crossover budget, non-thinking mode outperforms thinking mode at every tested budget, often by a wide margin} (Figure~\ref{fig:nothink-vs-thinking}).
The crossover---where thinking finally matches non-thinking---has not been reached at budgets $\leq$512 on GSM8K; on MATH-500, it shifts even further right, beyond 2048 tokens.
The headline result is stark: non-thinking mode with a 512-token budget achieves \textbf{93.1\%} accuracy using only 152 average tokens, while thinking mode with the \emph{same} budget reaches just \textbf{56.9\%}---requiring 477 average tokens.
This is not a marginal difference: non-thinking is \textbf{36.2 percentage points more accurate} while consuming \textbf{3.1$\times$ fewer tokens}.
Even at the smaller 256-token budget, non-thinking (87.5\%) outperforms thinking@512 (56.9\%).
On the harder MATH-500 benchmark, the tax is even larger: non-thinking@1024 achieves \textbf{59.8\%} vs.\ thinking@1024's \textbf{18.0\%}---a \textbf{41.8\,pp} gap.

The root cause is \emph{truncation waste}: thinking mode generates verbose reasoning chains that are truncated before reaching the answer.
While the mechanism may seem obvious in hindsight, three aspects of our findings are genuinely surprising.
\textbf{First, the magnitude}: a 69.5\,pp gap at budget 256 on GSM8K (41.8\,pp on MATH-500 at budget 1024) far exceeds what format overhead alone would predict---our decomposition reveals that 98.6\% of thinking responses are truncated at $b{=}256$, meaning the tax scales with the \emph{entire truncation rate}, not merely a format constant.
\textbf{Second, the tax amplification}: within the Qwen family, larger models get \emph{worse} at thinking under the same budget---the tax jumps from 36.2\,pp at 8B to ${\sim}$77\,pp at both 9B and 27B ($2.1\times$ amplification), consistent with longer reasoning chains that overflow the budget more severely.
\textbf{Third, the oracle}: natural stopping---when the model finishes its reasoning chain before exhausting the budget---predicts 99.0\% accuracy at zero compute cost, a novel signal not predicted by the truncation narrative alone.
At no tested budget ($\leq$512 on GSM8K, $\leq$2048 on MATH-500) has thinking mode reached parity with non-thinking, and on BIG-Bench Hard ($n{=}1{,}187$), the tax persists across non-mathematical tasks with task-specific crossovers.

\paragraph{The thinking tax amplifies with chain length.}
The problem is not confined to small models---within the Qwen family, it gets \emph{worse} as chains grow longer.
Across three model sizes (8B, 9B, 27B), thinking-mode accuracy at budget 512 \emph{collapses}: 56.9\% (8B), 15.5\% (9B), 18.3\% (27B)---while non-thinking accuracy remains uniformly high: 93.1\% (8B), 93.2\% (9B), 95.5\% (27B).
The resulting thinking tax is 36.2\,pp (8B), \textbf{77.7\,pp} (9B), and 77.2\,pp (27B)---a ${\sim}$2.1$\times$ amplification from 8B to 9B/27B.
The mechanism is chain-length driven: larger models generate more elaborate reasoning chains.
At budget 512, only \textbf{0.7\%} of 27B thinking traces produce a complete answer (vs.\ 37.4\% for 8B), meaning 99.3\% of responses are truncated and never reach a final answer.
Critically, the inefficiency is specific to thinking mode's format, not model capability: the 27B model in \emph{non-thinking} mode achieves 95.5\% at budget 512---the highest accuracy of any configuration---while its thinking mode manages only 18.3\%.
Within the Qwen family, this amplification has troubling deployment implications: as models produce longer chains, the budget threshold for thinking to break even shifts further right.

\paragraph{Natural stopping as an operational routing signal.}
Among samples where the reasoning chain naturally terminates within budget, accuracy is \textbf{99.0\%} (8B, budget 512).
This is not a post-hoc observation: whether the model emits EOS before exhausting \texttt{max\_new\_tokens} is a binary signal available at generation time, requiring no logit access or calibration.
\method{} uses this signal operationally in Stage~0 to route easy queries (natural stop $\Rightarrow$ accept immediately) vs.\ hard queries (budget exhausted $\Rightarrow$ escalate to decoupled answering).

\paragraph{The coupling tax and split-budget generation.}
The root cause of the thinking tax is architectural: reasoning and the final answer are \emph{coupled} in a single output stream, forcing them to compete for a shared token budget.
When the chain exceeds the budget, both the reasoning \emph{and} the answer are lost to truncation---even though a partial reasoning trace often contains enough information to derive the correct answer.
We call this the \couplingtax{}: the accuracy penalty imposed by coupling reasoning and answering under a shared budget.

The fix follows naturally: \emph{decouple} reasoning from answering via \splitbudget{}.
Generate a reasoning trace in thinking mode (budget $B_r$), then feed that trace---even if truncated---to a separate non-thinking pass (budget $B_a$) that extracts the final answer.
This simple architectural change eliminates truncation waste: the reasoning pass can use its full budget for thinking, and the answering pass can use its full budget for the answer.

\paragraph{\method{}: An exploratory probe.}
To test whether the \couplingtax{} can be mitigated at inference time, we instantiate split-budget generation as \method{} (\fullmethod{}), a training-free framework with three components:
(1)~a \emph{difficulty triage} via non-thinking probe that resolves easy queries immediately (${\sim}$89\% on GSM8K);
(2)~\emph{decoupled answer generation} that feeds the (possibly truncated) reasoning trace to a separate non-thinking pass, ensuring reasoning tokens never crowd out the answer; and
(3)~\emph{iterative refinement} where subsequent thinking rounds receive the previous answer as a hint, with convergence-based stopping.
On GSM8K ($n{=}200$), \method{} achieves \textbf{94.0\%} at 235 avg tokens---+5.0\,pp over nothink@256 with zero unique losses.
Crucially, the core mechanism---\emph{decoupled answer generation}---recovers +25.4\,pp over standard thinking on hard samples.
Full-scale MATH-500 evaluation ($n{=}500$, same hardware as baselines) confirms the gains definitively: IRIS@2048 achieves \textbf{67.2\%} and IRIS@4096 achieves \textbf{74.0\%}---exceeding nothink@1024 (59.8\%) by +7.4\,pp and +14.2\,pp respectively, with 95\% CI lower bounds above 59.8\%.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
Our work makes four contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{The Coupling Tax} (\S\ref{sec:thinking-tax}).
    We identify and quantify the \couplingtax{} in models with architecturally distinct thinking and non-thinking modes: coupling reasoning and answering under a shared token budget causes accuracy to collapse via truncation waste.
    Non-thinking mode outperforms thinking mode at every tested budget ($\leq$2048) on GSM8K and MATH-500, across three Qwen-family model sizes, with the tax amplifying on harder benchmarks and larger models.
    The phenomenon extends beyond mathematics: on five BIG-Bench Hard subtasks ($n{=}1{,}187$, \S\ref{sec:finding:bbh}), the tax persists across task types---from +36.8\,pp on boolean expressions to +88.0\,pp on object tracking at budget 256---with task-dependent crossovers ranging from ${\sim}$512 to ${>}$2048, as predicted by our decomposition.
    Cross-architecture experiments on DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B confirm that truncation waste is a structural property of coupled generation, while the end-to-end tax magnitude depends on availability of a genuinely distinct non-thinking mode (Appendix~\ref{app:deepseek}).

    \item \textbf{Truncation-Waste Decomposition} (\S\ref{sec:theory}).
    A predictive decomposition expressing thinking-mode accuracy as $\alpha_c \cdot F_L(b) + \alpha_t \cdot (1 - F_L(b))$, yielding closed-form predictions for the crossover budget.
    We formalize the coupling constraint $|Z|{+}|A|{\le}b$ and derive the \emph{recoverable coupling tax}---the accuracy gain achievable by decoupling reasoning from answering (Proposition~\ref{prop:recoverable-tax}).
    Held-out prediction tests confirm the framework generalizes across budgets and benchmarks.

    \item \textbf{Tax Amplification with Chain Length (Within Qwen Family)} (\S\ref{sec:finding:model-size}).
    Within three Qwen-family checkpoints, the tax worsens sharply: 36.2\,pp at 8B vs.\ ${\sim}$77\,pp at both 9B and 27B (a $2.1\times$ amplification), consistent with stochastic dominance of chain-length distributions (Proposition~\ref{prop:inverse-scaling})---while non-thinking accuracy remains uniformly high (93.1\%--95.5\%).

    \item \textbf{Proof-of-Concept Mitigation via Split-Budget Generation} (\S\ref{sec:method}, \S\ref{sec:experiments}).
    We instantiate \method{} (\fullmethod{}), a training-free framework that decouples reasoning from answering.
    \method{} improves over nothink@$B_1$ on GSM8K (+5.0\,pp, $n{=}200$), where nothink accuracy has saturated.
    On MATH-500, full-scale evaluation ($n{=}500$, same hardware) confirms that IRIS exceeds the nothink ceiling: IRIS@4096 achieves \textbf{74.0\%} \ci{70.0}{77.7}---surpassing nothink@1024 (59.8\%) by +14.2\,pp.
\end{enumerate}
