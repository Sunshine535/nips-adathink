Before introducing our method, we present a systematic empirical study of
the costs and failure modes of chain-of-thought reasoning under constrained
token budgets.  All experiments in this section use
\textbf{Qwen3-8B}~\cite{qwen3_2025} with its native thinking mode
(\texttt{enable\_thinking=True}), with \textbf{Qwen3.5-9B} and
\textbf{Qwen3.5-27B} for cross-scale validation,
evaluated on the full GSM8K test set ($n{=}1{,}319$)
unless otherwise noted.
We control test-time compute via the \texttt{max\_new\_tokens} parameter,
which caps the \emph{total} number of newly generated tokens---including
both the thinking trace (\texttt{<think>...<\!/think>}) and the final answer.
Crucially, thinking mode and non-thinking mode operate under the
\emph{same} total output-token budget~$b$, ensuring a fair comparison:
\texttt{think@$b$} must fit its entire reasoning chain \emph{plus}
its answer within $b$~tokens, while \texttt{nothink@$b$} uses all
$b$~tokens for the answer directly.
Non-thinking results are obtained by setting
\texttt{enable\_thinking=False} in the Qwen3 chat template, which
suppresses the thinking block entirely.
Greedy decoding ($\tau{=}0$) is used throughout; we deliberately scope to the deterministic setting to isolate the truncation mechanism from sampling variance (the interaction between the thinking tax and stochastic decoding, including best-of-$K$ and self-consistency baselines, is an orthogonal question).
When thinking mode exhausts its budget without producing a final answer,
a short \emph{projection pass} (up to 32 additional tokens) is applied to
extract a numerical answer from the truncated output
(details in Appendix~\ref{app:experimental_details}).
This is \emph{favorable} to thinking mode: without projection,
think@512 accuracy drops from 56.9\% to ${<}$6\%, making
our reported thinking tax a conservative estimate.

Our five findings collectively establish that (i)~thinking mode imposes a
severe \emph{tax} at practical token budgets, (ii)~models already emit
reliable signals indicating when this tax is unnecessary, and
(iii)~a large fraction of compute is wasted on questions the model
cannot solve regardless of budget.
These findings motivate the design of \method{} (\fullmethod{}) in
\S\ref{sec:method}.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Finding 1: Non-Thinking Beats Thinking at All Matched Budgets}
\label{sec:finding:tax}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Our most striking result is that, when the output-token budget is held
constant, \emph{non-thinking mode uniformly dominates thinking mode}
across every budget level we tested.
Table~\ref{tab:thinking-tax} summarizes the comparison.

\input{../results/paper_figures/table_thinking_tax_main}

On the complete GSM8K test set ($n{=}1{,}319$),
\texttt{nothink@256} achieves \textbf{87.5\%} vs.\ only
\textbf{18.0\%} for \texttt{think@256}---a \textbf{69.5\,pp} gap.
At the 27B scale, the gap is even starker: nothink@512 = 95.5\% vs.\ think@512 = 18.3\% ($+77.2$\,pp).
Non-thinking mode saturates at moderate budgets, while thinking has not reached parity at any tested budget (Table~\ref{tab:thinking-tax}).

The root cause is that thinking mode must fit its reasoning chain \emph{plus} its answer within $b$ tokens; at low budgets, the chain consumes all available tokens.
At $b{=}256$, 88.8\% of non-thinking samples terminate naturally, vs.\ less than 2\% for thinking (Table~\ref{tab:thinking-tax}).
This does not imply CoT is useless---above the crossover budget (${\sim}$2048 on GSM8K; higher on harder benchmarks), thinking recovers and can surpass non-thinking---but below this threshold, the reasoning overhead is a \emph{tax} that must be justified by problem difficulty.

\paragraph{Failure-mode decomposition.}
Table~\ref{tab:failure-decomp} decomposes the thinking tax into its constituent
failure modes, providing causal evidence for the truncation mechanism.
At $b{=}256$, \textbf{98.6\%} of thinking responses are truncated before
reaching a final answer, compared to only \textbf{11.2\%} in non-thinking mode.
Among the rare natural-stop thinking samples (1.4\%), accuracy is 100\%---the
model can reason correctly when given sufficient budget, but the chain
format prevents it from finishing.
At $b{=}512$, natural-stop rate rises to 38.0\% with 99.0\% accuracy among
completers; the truncated 62.0\% achieve only 32.2\%, dragging overall
accuracy to 56.9\%.

\subsection{Finding 2: Natural Stop Is a Free Confidence Oracle}
\label{sec:finding:natural-stop}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

When a thinking model generates a complete reasoning chain and emits
a ``Final answer'' marker \emph{before} exhausting its token budget,
we say the sample exhibits a \emph{natural stop}.
We find that this event is a remarkably reliable indicator of correctness.

\input{../results/paper_figures/table_natural_stop_oracle}

At $b{=}512$ with Qwen3-8B, 37.4\% of samples terminate before consuming
95\% of their token budget---we call these \emph{natural stops}
(Table~\ref{tab:natural-stop-oracle}).
Among these natural-stop samples, accuracy is \textbf{99.0\%}---nearly perfect.
Among samples that exhaust the budget (i.e., are truncated), accuracy is
only 31.8\%, yielding an accuracy gap of \textbf{$+$67.2\,pp}
(Figure~\ref{fig:natural-stop-split}, Table~\ref{tab:natural-stop-oracle}).
A stricter definition---requiring an explicit ``Final answer'' marker
(\texttt{has\_final})---selects only 6.1\% of samples but achieves
93.8\% accuracy, further confirming the signal's reliability.

Unlike soft confidence scores, the natural-stop signal is
\emph{binary}, \emph{endogenous}, and \emph{free}---it requires no logit access, hidden states, or calibration.
The effect generalizes: DeepSeek-R1-8B shows similar behavior (Table~\ref{tab:natural-stop-oracle}), with natural-stop rates increasing steadily with budget on MATH-500 (31.2\% $\to$ 76.2\% as $b$ scales from 1024 to 4096).

\subsection{Finding 4: The Thinking Tax Scales with Model Size}
\label{sec:finding:model-size}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

One might hope that the thinking tax diminishes with more capable models.
We test this hypothesis by comparing Qwen3-8B, Qwen3.5-9B, and
Qwen3.5-27B in thinking mode at identical budgets.
The results are counterintuitive.

\input{../results/paper_figures/table_model_size_scaling}

At $b{=}512$, thinking-mode accuracy \emph{collapses dramatically}
with model size: 8B achieves 56.9\%, 9B drops to 15.5\%, and 27B
reaches only 18.3\% (Table~\ref{tab:model-size-scaling}).
Non-thinking accuracy remains uniformly high: 8B = 93.1\%, 9B = 93.2\%, 27B = 95.5\%.
The resulting thinking tax is 36.2\,pp (8B), \textbf{77.7\,pp} (9B), and 77.2\,pp (27B)---a \textbf{2.1$\times$} amplification from 8B to 9B.

The root cause is that larger models produce longer chains: at $b{=}512$, the 27B model's natural-stop rate is just \textbf{0.7\%} (vs.\ 8B's 37.4\%), so 99.3\% of its responses are truncated.
Crucially, the 27B model in \emph{non-thinking} mode achieves \textbf{95.5\%} at the same budget---the \emph{highest accuracy of any configuration}---confirming that model capability is intact and the tax is purely a format overhead.
Even quadrupling the budget does not close the gap: at $b{=}2048$, the 9B model reaches only 66.8\% in thinking mode---still below 9B \texttt{nothink@512} (93.2\%) by 26.4\,pp.
The tax does not diminish with scale---it \emph{worsens}, making adaptive allocation increasingly important for frontier models.

\subsection{Finding 5: The Tax Generalizes Beyond Mathematics}
\label{sec:finding:bbh}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

A natural concern is whether the thinking tax is specific to mathematical
reasoning.
We test this on five BIG-Bench Hard (BBH)~\citep{suzgun2023challenging}
subtasks spanning symbolic reasoning, causal inference, temporal reasoning,
and object tracking ($n{=}1{,}187$ total, Qwen3-8B).
None involve mathematical computation.

crossover between budgets 1024--2048.
Per-task crossovers vary from ${\sim}$512 (boolean expressions) to
${>}$2048 (causal judgement), matching the theory's prediction that
$b^*$ depends on chain-length distributions.}

Table~\ref{tab:bbh-main} shows the aggregate and two extreme subtasks.
At budget 256, nothink achieves 49.9\% vs.\ thinking's 16.6\%---a
+33.3\,pp tax, confirming the phenomenon is not math-specific.
The per-task variation is striking:
\texttt{tracking\_shuffled\_objects} exhibits an \textbf{88.0\,pp} tax at
budget 256 (nothink 88.8\% vs.\ thinking 0.8\%), while
\texttt{causal\_judgement} shows a persistent tax even at budget 2048
(+3.7\,pp)---thinking has not reached parity at any tested budget.
This variation in crossover budgets is exactly what our theory predicts
(Proposition~\ref{prop:crossover}): $b^*$ is determined by the chain-length
CDF, which varies with task complexity.
Full per-task results are in Appendix~\ref{app:bbh}.

A difficulty decomposition (Appendix~\ref{app:impossible}) shows that
31.8\% of GSM8K problems remain unsolved by thinking mode at budgets
up to $b{=}512$, establishing a practical ceiling on thinking-mode
accuracy in this regime and motivating the routing approach of
\S\ref{sec:method}.

\paragraph{Summary.}
Our findings paint a coherent picture:
(1)~non-thinking dominates at all matched budgets;
(2)~natural stop predicts 99.0\% accuracy at zero cost;
(3)~token utilization collapses at high budgets (Appendix~\ref{app:utilization});
(4)~the tax worsens with model scale;
(5)~the tax generalizes to non-mathematical reasoning (BBH); and
(6)~31.8\% of problems are beyond reach at any budget (Appendix~\ref{app:impossible}).
The natural question is: can we \emph{think only when thinking helps}?
This is the goal of \method{}, introduced next.
