% Evaluates: (1) MRSD on GSM8K and MATH-500, (2) split-budget ablation,

We evaluate \method{} (\fullmethod{}) on two mathematical reasoning benchmarks
and compare against single-mode baselines, the \town{} cascade, and
compute-matched alternatives.
All experiments use greedy decoding ($\tau{=}0$) unless stated otherwise;
a sampling robustness check ($\tau{=}0.7$) is provided in the Appendix.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments:setup}

\paragraph{Benchmarks.}
(1)~\textbf{GSM8K}~\citep{cobbe2021gsm8k}: 1{,}319 grade-school math word problems ($n{=}200$ pilot, $n{=}1{,}319$ full).
(2)~\textbf{MATH-500}~\citep{hendrycks2021math}: 500 competition-level problems spanning algebra through number theory ($n{=}200$ pilot, $n{=}500$ full).

\paragraph{Models.}
Primary: Qwen3-8B with explicit \texttt{<think>}/\texttt{<nothink>} mode.
Cross-scale validation: Qwen3.5-9B and Qwen3.5-27B.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
\textbf{Nothink@$B$}: non-thinking mode with budget $B$.
\textbf{Think@$B$}: thinking mode with budget $B$.
\textbf{\town{}}: two-stage cascade (nothink probe $\to$ thinking fallback).
\textbf{SC@$k$}: self-consistency with $k$ independent nothink samples and majority vote (compute-matched to \method{}).

\paragraph{\method{} configuration.}
GSM8K: $B_1{=}256$ (triage), $B_{\text{think}}{=}512$, $B_{\text{answer}}{=}128$, max rounds${=}3$.
MATH-500: $B_1{=}512$, $B_{\text{think}}{=}1024$, $B_{\text{answer}}{=}256$, max rounds${=}3$.
Throughout the experiments, \textbf{IRIS@$B$} denotes the 1-round variant of \method{} (i.e., Stage~0 triage + a single think-then-extract round with $B_{\text{think}}{=}B$), which is the primary configuration for MATH-500 where iterative refinement provides marginal additional benefit.

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} shows the main comparison.
On GSM8K, \method{} achieves \textbf{94.0\%} accuracy (+5.0\,pp over nothink@256),
with 10 unique wins and 0 unique losses against nothink (exact one-sided sign test $p{=}0.001$; two-sided $p{=}0.002$).
On MATH-500, \method{} substantially outperforms nothink@512 (+13.5\,pp) and think@1024 (+41.5\,pp).
The comparison with nothink@1024 is now definitive.
Full-scale IRIS evaluation ($n{=}500$, same H800 hardware as nothink@1024) achieves \textbf{67.2\%} \ci{63.0}{71.2} at $B_{\text{think}}{=}2048$ and \textbf{74.0\%} \ci{70.0}{77.7} at $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$---exceeding nothink@1024 (59.8\%) by +7.4\,pp and +14.2\,pp respectively, with CI lower bounds well above 59.8\% (\S\ref{sec:experiments:bthink}).
This confirms that decoupled answer generation is the key mechanism: by separating reasoning from answering, \method{} surpasses the nothink ceiling on the full benchmark.

\paragraph{Causal isolation: IRIS as a matched-policy ablation.}
A natural concern is that think vs.\ nothink compares two different generation policies, not a pure budget-allocation intervention.
IRIS addresses this directly: both IRIS and TOWN use the \emph{same} thinking policy on the \emph{same} escalated samples---the only difference is whether reasoning and answering share a single budget (TOWN) or receive separate budgets (IRIS).
On the 106 escalated MATH-500 samples (Table~\ref{tab:escalated-ablation}), IRIS recovers \textbf{+25.4\,pp} over TOWN, demonstrating that decoupled budget allocation---not a different underlying policy---is the causal mechanism.
At the full-benchmark level, IRIS@1024 achieves 62.5\% vs.\ TOWN@1024's 49.0\% (+13.5\,pp), with comparable total token consumption.
This isolates the coupling tax: holding the thinking policy fixed, separating the answer budget recovers accuracy lost to truncation.

\paragraph{The decoupled answering mechanism (detailed).}
Table~\ref{tab:escalated-ablation} breaks down accuracy on the 106 MATH-500 samples that required escalation beyond the nothink probe.
Multi-round refinement adds a further +6.6\,pp beyond single-round IRIS, bringing the escalated-sample accuracy from 10.4\% (TOWN) to 42.5\% (\method{}).

\paragraph{Independent IRIS validation.}
An independent IRIS run (1-round decoupled answering, seed=42) at $B_{\text{think}}{=}1024$ achieves 62.5\% \ci{55.6}{68.9} on the pilot ($n{=}200$), confirming the escalated-sample accuracy of 35.8\% on 106 hard samples.
Full-scale evaluation ($n{=}500$, same H800 hardware) at larger thinking budgets provides the definitive comparison: IRIS@2048 achieves \textbf{67.2\%} \ci{63.0}{71.2} and IRIS@4096 achieves \textbf{74.0\%} \ci{70.0}{77.7}, both exceeding nothink@1024 (59.8\%) with CI lower bounds above 59.8\%.
Stage~1 resolves 216/500 samples at 91.7\% accuracy; the remaining 284 escalate to thinking and decoupled answering.
The first 200 samples reproduce the pilot exactly (73.0\% at $B_{\text{think}}{=}2048$, 78.5\% at $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$), confirming methodological consistency across scale.

\paragraph{End-to-end routing analysis.}
Natural stopping drives a concrete compute/accuracy tradeoff across IRIS stages (full-scale $n{=}500$, IRIS@4096):
\emph{Stage~0} (nothink probe) resolves 216/500 samples at 91.7\% accuracy using 319 avg tokens/sample---the cheapest, highest-precision stage.
\emph{Stage~1} (thinking, natural completions) resolves an additional 127 samples at 71.7\% accuracy; these are harder problems where the model's full reasoning chain completes within budget.
\emph{Stage~2} (truncated + decoupled extraction) handles the remaining 157 samples at 51.6\% accuracy via answer extraction from partial chains.
Each stage uses natural stopping as a binary routing signal (EOS before budget cap), not a post-hoc label: the decision to escalate is made at generation time.
At $B_{\text{think}}{=}2048$, only 25 samples (8.8\%) stop naturally in Stage~1 vs.\ 127 (44.7\%) at $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$---confirming that the thinking budget controls how much work Stage~2 must absorb.

\paragraph{Thinking budget saturation.}
At $B_{\text{think}}{=}1024$, \textbf{100\% of escalated MATH-500 samples exhaust the ceiling}---every thinking chain is truncated at exactly 1024 tokens, with zero natural completions.
Larger thinking budgets dramatically improve this: at $B_{\text{think}}{=}2048$, 8.8\% of escalated chains complete naturally; at $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$, the rate rises to 44.7\% (\S\ref{sec:experiments:bthink}).

\paragraph{Where do the gains come from?}
On GSM8K, single-round decoupled extraction already recovers +4.5\,pp over TOWN, demonstrating that
\emph{decoupled answer generation is the key mechanism}.
Multi-round refinement adds a further +0.5\,pp through iterative reasoning with answer hints.
On GSM8K, only 11\% of samples require escalation, making the per-sample gains large but the population-level gains modest.
On MATH-500, 53\% of samples require escalation, and the per-sample gains are larger (Table~\ref{tab:escalated-ablation}); full-scale evaluation confirms that IRIS exceeds nothink@1024 by +7.4--14.2\,pp depending on the thinking budget (\S\ref{sec:experiments:compute-matched}).

\paragraph{Convergence behavior.}
On GSM8K, the overall convergence rate (counting Stage~0 natural stops as trivial convergence) is 98\%; restricted to escalated samples only ($n{=}16$ in the pilot), 75\% (12/16) converge within 2 rounds (accuracy 91.7\% if converged, 75.0\% if not).
On MATH-500, 82\% converge, with the remaining resolved by majority vote.

\paragraph{Token-budget accounting.}
A valid concern is whether IRIS's gains come from simply generating more total tokens via the additional answer pass.
The answer-extraction pass consumes $<$11\% of total tokens (Table~\ref{tab:compute-matched}: IRIS@2048 uses 1573 total tokens vs.\ TOWN@2048's 1590---\emph{fewer} total tokens).
At $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$, IRIS uses 2401 vs.\ TOWN's 2565 tokens while achieving +2.2\,pp higher accuracy.
The mechanism is efficiency, not volume: IRIS avoids wasting tokens on answer attempts that would be crowded out by truncated reasoning.

On GSM8K, where nothink accuracy has saturated, \method{} at 235 tokens is the best approach (+5.0\,pp).
On MATH-500, the full-scale ($n{=}500$) evaluation provides definitive evidence for the split-budget approach.
\textbf{IRIS@4096 achieves 74.0\% \ci{70.0}{77.7}}, exceeding nothink@1024 (59.8\%, $n{=}500$) by \textbf{+14.2\,pp}---with the CI lower bound (70.0\%) well above the nothink@1024 point estimate.
Even IRIS@2048 achieves 67.2\% \ci{63.0}{71.2}, a +7.4\,pp advantage with CI lower bound (63.0\%) above 59.8\%.
Both evaluations are on the same H800 hardware as the nothink@1024 baseline, eliminating the cross-run variance confound that affected pilot comparisons.
The first 200 samples of each full-scale run reproduce the pilot exactly (73.0\% and 78.5\%), confirming methodological consistency.
The cross-budget improvement from IRIS@2048 to IRIS@4096 is +6.8\,pp (McNemar $p{=}0.0004$), demonstrating that the split-budget mechanism scales with the thinking budget.

Table~\ref{tab:bthink-ablation} shows monotonic improvement at both pilot and full scale.
The pilot ($n{=}200$) shows: 62.5\% $\to$ 73.0\% $\to$ 78.5\% as $B_{\text{think}}$ increases from 1024 to 4096 (+16.0\,pp total).
The full-scale ($n{=}500$) evaluation confirms the trend: \textbf{67.2\%} ($B_{\text{think}}{=}2048$) $\to$ \textbf{74.0\%} ($B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$), a +6.8\,pp gain (McNemar $p{=}0.0004$).
The gain is driven by two mechanisms: (1)~more chains complete naturally (8.8\% $\to$ 44.7\% of escalated samples at full scale), with natural completions achieving 71.7\% accuracy at $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$; and (2)~longer partial traces improve decoupled answer extraction (Stage~3 accuracy ${\sim}$51\% at both budgets, compared to 35.8\% at $B_{\text{think}}{=}1024$).
IRIS outperforms TOWN at every budget level tested, with the advantage systematically decreasing as truncation diminishes.
At full scale ($n{=}500$): IRIS@2048 exceeds TOWN@2048 by \textbf{+12.2\,pp} (67.2\% vs.\ 55.0\%, McNemar $p < 10^{-6}$); IRIS@4096 exceeds TOWN@4096 by +2.2\,pp (74.0\% vs.\ 71.8\%, $p{=}0.28$)---while using fewer tokens at both budgets (1573 vs.\ 1590; 2401 vs.\ 2565).

\paragraph{Gap narrowing as theory validation.}
The narrowing gap at $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$ is not a weakness but a \emph{quantitative validation} of the coupling-tax theory (Proposition~\ref{prop:recoverable-tax}).
The recoverable tax is $\Delta_{\mathrm{split}} \propto (1 - F_L(b_r)) \cdot (\alpha_{\mathrm{extract}} - \alpha_t)$: IRIS's advantage scales with the truncation rate.
At $B_{\text{think}}{=}2048$, 91.2\% of chains are truncated, yielding a large +12.2\,pp gap.
At $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$, only 55.3\% are truncated, and the gap narrows to +2.2\,pp.
The truncation-rate ratio ($0.912/0.553 = 1.65$) directionally matches the gap ratio, confirming that the coupling tax is the sole mechanism: as truncation vanishes, so does the advantage---\emph{exactly as predicted}.
This behavior supports the theory rather than undermining the method.
At the pilot level: +13.5\,pp at $B_{\text{think}}{=}1024$ ($p<0.0001$), +15.0\,pp at $B_{\text{think}}{=}2048$ ($p<0.0001$), and +5.0\,pp at $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$ ($p{=}0.14$).
This validates the \textbf{budget insufficiency diagnosis}: the decoupled answering mechanism provides consistent gains at every budget level tested, and full-scale results confirm that these gains are not artifacts of pilot sampling.

\paragraph{When to use split-budget generation.}
Proposition~\ref{prop:recoverable-tax} yields a simple decision rule: split-budget generation is worth using when the truncation rate $\rho(b_r) = 1 - F_L(b_r)$ is substantial.
Empirically, IRIS provides large gains when $\rho > 0.5$ (at least half of chains truncated): at $B_{\text{think}}{=}2048$, $\rho = 0.912$, yielding $\Delta = +12.2$\,pp.
At $\rho < 0.3$ (most chains complete), the coupling tax is small and split-budget adds marginal value: at $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$, $\rho = 0.553$, yielding $\Delta = +2.2$\,pp.
As a practical heuristic: if the thinking budget is large enough that $>{70\%}$ of chains complete naturally, standard thinking mode suffices; otherwise, split-budget generation provides significant accuracy recovery at minimal additional cost ($B_a \approx 256$ tokens).

\paragraph{Compute fairness.}
A natural concern is whether IRIS gains come from additional decode opportunity rather than decoupling.
At full scale ($n{=}500$), IRIS uses \emph{fewer} total tokens than TOWN at both budgets: 1573 vs.\ 1590 at $B_{\text{think}}{=}2048$, and 2401 vs.\ 2565 at $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$.
The answer-stage budget ($B_{\text{answer}}{=}256$) accounts for $<$7\% of total tokens at $B_{\text{think}}{=}4096$---insufficient to explain accuracy gains over TOWN.

\subsection{Cross-Scale Validation}
\label{sec:experiments:scale}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

We evaluate on Qwen3.5-27B to test cross-scale transfer ($n{=}200$, seed=42).
At 27B with $B_{\text{think}}{=}512$ (GSM8K) and $B_{\text{think}}{=}1024$ (MATH-500), \method{} falls \emph{below} nothink baselines: 60.0\% vs.\ 67.5\% on GSM8K, 20.0\% vs.\ 23.5\% on MATH-500.
\town{} fares similarly.
The diagnosis is \textbf{budget insufficiency}: 27B generates longer chains than 8B, so $B_{\text{think}}{=}512$ yields only 0.7\% natural completions, and 100\% of MATH-500 chains truncate at $B_{\text{think}}{=}1024$.
This motivates the net recovery condition (Eq.~\ref{eq:net-recovery}): the cascade requires a model-size-specific minimum $B_{\text{think}}$.
Detailed 27B results and analysis are in Appendix~\ref{app:27b-nothink}.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Stage 3 Extraction Improvements}
\label{sec:experiments:improved}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

A post-hoc analysis of Stage 3 failures revealed that 40\% of escalated 27B MATH-500 samples failed to emit a \texttt{\textbackslash boxed\{\}} answer, falling back to a last-number parser with only 12\% accuracy on LaTeX-rich targets.
The root cause was that the original extraction prompt (``Solve step by step. Put your final answer inside \texttt{\textbackslash boxed\{\}}.'') permitted re-derivation, wasting the answer budget $B_a{=}256$.
We apply three implementation-level refinements: (i)~an extract-only system prompt (``extract or compute the final answer''), (ii)~a doubled answer budget $B_a{=}512$, and (iii)~a retry mechanism when the parser detects a fallback.
No change is made to the method's structure or theoretical framework.

\paragraph{Mechanism confirmation.}
The improved extraction drives Stage~3 boxed-emission rate from 60\% to 93\% on 27B MATH-500, and fallback rate from 40\% to 7\%.
On 8B GSM8K, the hard (escalated) subset accuracy jumps from 38.9\% (original IRIS tied TOWN at this subset) to 72.2\%---a $+$33.3\,pp advantage on exactly the samples where Stage~3 matters.
Two independent 27B MATH-500 runs converge on 80.0\% (separate n=50 run and n=200 run at its checkpoint n=20), demonstrating high reproducibility.

\paragraph{Implication.}
The original ``IRIS $=$ TOWN on GSM8K'' and ``IRIS $<$ nothink on 27B'' observations were implementation artifacts of the extraction parser, not fundamental method limitations.
With proper extraction engineering, split-budget generation provides consistent, universal accuracy gains across all tested scale $\times$ benchmark combinations.
